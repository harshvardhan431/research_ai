TITLE: A Look at Advanced Learners' Use of Mobile Devices for English Language Study: Insights from Interview Data
AUTHORS: Mariusz Kruk, University of Zielona Gora, Poland
VENUE: The EUROCALL Review, Volume 25, No. 2
YEAR: 2017

ABSTRACT
The paper reports a study exploring how advanced learners of English engage with their mobile devices to build learning experiences that meet their needs and goals as foreign language learners. Data were collected from 20 students via semi-structured interviews and analyzed both qualitatively and quantitatively. Findings showed a mixed picture: some students showed strong awareness of the benefits of mobile devices for finding tools and information suited to their personal learning style, while others used their devices in a fairly intuitive, ad hoc way, especially in the classroom.

PROBLEM
Mobile devices are widely used by language learners, but the literature lacked clarity on how advanced learners specifically integrate mobile devices into their broader learning autonomy. Prior researchers (Reinders and White, 2016) argued that future research into technology-mediated learner autonomy needs to be closely tied to the actual tools, settings, and activities that matter to learners, rather than treated as a generic feature of language learning technology. There was limited work capturing, in learners' own words, whether and how mobile device use actually helps them meet their personal language-learning needs and goals.

PROPOSED SOLUTION / RESEARCH QUESTION
The study reframes an open question from Reinders and White's overview into a specific research question: do students engage with their mobile devices to build learning experiences that meet their needs and goals as English learners, and if so, why and how. The approach taken was qualitative and exploratory rather than proposing a new tool or system — it investigates existing learner behavior through interviews rather than introducing an intervention.

METHOD
Participants were 20 Polish university students of English philology across BA and MA years, with proficiency levels ranging roughly from B1 to C2 on the CEFR scale. Data were gathered through semi-structured interviews using a fixed set of guiding questions (e.g. which devices they use, why, how often, in or out of class, which apps, and whether device use increased their study time), while allowing learners to elaborate freely. Interviews were partially transcribed, then coded and recoded for recurring themes, and additionally analyzed quantitatively by counting response frequencies and percentages.

FINDINGS
Six thematic categories emerged: usage of mobile devices, reasons for using them, resources and tools, mobile encounters (when/where devices are used), language skills practiced, and study performance. Smartphones were the most commonly used device, with average usage of about 3.8 years for English study. Convenience, speed, and constant internet access were the main reasons cited for use. Commonly used tools included online dictionaries, Google Translate, Duolingo, and vocabulary flashcard apps like Fiszkoteka. Most students used devices more in leisure time than in class, and mainly for informal rather than formal learning. Vocabulary was overwhelmingly the most practiced skill; grammar, listening, reading and speaking were rarely targeted via mobile devices. About 75% of interviewees felt mobile device use increased the time and effectiveness of their English study, while 25% were unsure.

DISCUSSION
The overall picture was cautiously positive: all participants used mobile devices to some degree for autonomous English learning, and most linked this to easy access to resources and flexibility. However, mobile device use was concentrated almost entirely on vocabulary, with little engagement for grammar, listening, speaking, or writing, indicating a narrow rather than well-rounded use of the technology. Device use in the classroom was also largely unprompted by teachers — none of the interviewees reported teachers actively recommending apps or designing mobile-based tasks, suggesting a gap between the technology's potential and how it is currently leveraged pedagogically.

LIMITATIONS AND GAPS
The authors explicitly flag several limitations that double as directions for future research: (1) the sample was small (20 students), limiting generalizability; (2) the sample was homogenous — all from the same institution and same subject area (English philology), so results may not transfer to other learner populations; (3) the study relied on a single semi-structured interview per participant rather than repeated interviews over time (e.g. across an academic year), which the authors suggest could yield richer, more detailed data; (4) the study did not examine teacher-side interventions, i.e. what would happen if teachers actively integrated mobile apps and tasks into instruction rather than leaving device use entirely learner-driven. These are direct openings for follow-up research: larger and more diverse samples, longitudinal designs, and teacher-intervention studies that test whether guided mobile task design improves the currently narrow (vocabulary-only) skill coverage.
